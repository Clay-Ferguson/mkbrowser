## Glossary 

### Confederation

Definition: A political system characterized by a loose association of sovereign states that retain their individual independence and delegate only limited, specific powers to a central authority. In the Federalist Papers, the term is primarily used to describe the Articles of {Confederation}, which the authors critiqued as a "league of friendship" rather than a true government. This form of "confederacy" was argued to be fundamentally flawed because it lacked the power to tax, regulate commerce, or enforce laws directly upon citizens, leading to national weakness and the threat of anarchy.

### UNION

The political and legal framework establishing a single, sovereign federal republic composed of the several American states. In the Federalist Papers, the Union is characterized not as a loose confederation of independent entities, but as a "firm Union" endowed with the energy and authority to govern individuals directly. It is presented as the essential mechanism for ensuring domestic tranquility, providing for a common defense, and preventing the rise of competing regional factions that would otherwise lead to the dissolution of the states into smaller, weaker confederacies.

### Constitution

Definition: The supreme law of the United States, established to replace the Articles of {Confederation} with a more "energetic" and stable national government. In the Federalist Papers, it is defended as a transformative social contract that balances a robust federal authority with preserved state sovereignty. It operates on the principles of separation of powers, checks and balances, and federalism, designed specifically to prevent the accumulation of all powers—legislative, executive, and judiciary—in the same hands, which the authors define as the very definition of tyranny.
